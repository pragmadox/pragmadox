Introduction

Jay Price is a veteran of the United States Marine Corps where he worked as an Arabic Linguist and Intelligence Analyst supporting missions in the Middle East and Africa. In 2014, He completed his Bachelor of Arts in Political Science and Asian Languages and Civilizations, with certificates in Middle East Studies and International Relations .

After interning with the House of Representatives and Stroz Friedberg, he completed his Master of Science in Data Analytics at Fordham University in New York in 2017.

His interests include unmanned aerial vehicles, photography, running, skydiving, web development, and data analysis.

    Phenomena Explained

    August 14, 2017


    On the way home from a wedding in Maine, I stopped to take a piss. While I sat there somewhere in South Maine relieving myself, I wistfully noted the many instances of n***** and Fuck Obama and many strange, very conflicted testaments to the sexual prowess of homosexual men. Some days 2017 feels too similar to 1965.

    Those of us who want to pass on a better world to our children definitely still have much work to do. I can only credit the countless wonderful patient, insightful friends, the military, and academia with helping me to overcome the provincial attitudes that would almost certainly have defined my outlook. American tax dollars took me out of the world of the military and offered me an opportunity to learn and grow, unconstrained by the small-town mentality in which I'd been born and raised.

    Education is not only about learning complex ways of counting things in a database, it is for learning how to be a better person in a world where every intuition is to align oneself with people who think, look, and feel like you. Our ongoing challenge is to let the sunlight disinfect the memetic viruses that periodically infect entire groups within our society.

    Racism is real in 2017. I wish there was a nicer way to put this, but the years ahead, as the technological revolution splits our country at the seams and exposes the starkness of the divides between us, it offers opportunities for policymakers, complicit or indifferent cogniscenti, and politicians to exploit these miniscule differences for their own gains. This will almost certainly be a uniquely difficult time for our country and the world.

    On the bright side, there is so much that we can do, so much left to do, so many opportunities to reinvigorate community, learn together, live together, build together, die together.

    “The King will reply, 'Truly I tell you, whatever you did for one of the least of these brothers and sisters of mine, you did for me.'

    رَبِّ اغْفِرْ لِقَوْمِي فَإِنَّهُمْ لاَ يَعْلَمُونَ

    After all, we're really not that different.


    August 10, 2017

    Sitting around the campire, I struck up a conversations with an older gentlemen. He was well-traveled, had worked for NASA and consulted on projects for air traffic control and radar systems. I mentioned in passing that I would soon be starting a video blog to discuss the current state of affairs in the world and hoping to inspire a more comprehensive, patient, and meaningful discussion about what aspects of society could be altered to create greater equality and address the short-comings that exist in terms of creating a more sustainable social order. He asked me, "What do you intend to call your blog?" I responded, "A Close-Reading of Politics." He laughed and said, "You'll never get an audience with a blog that is essential 'Phenomena Explained.'" I laughed. I am sincerely hoping that he is wrong.
